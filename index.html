Research

My research examines the frictions — asymmetric information, moral hazard, and contracting limitations — that distort financial markets and corporate decisions. Most of my work asks a simple question: when the people designing, trading, or holding financial contracts don't have the same information or incentives, what goes wrong, and what can be done about it?

Mortgage Markets & Adverse Selection

When lenders sell loans, what do buyers really get? I study how asymmetric information shapes which mortgages get securitized, when they're sold, and how reputations discipline the market.

Corporate Finance & Incentives

How should firms pay managers when investment opportunities change over time? My work on dynamic agency explores the tension between giving managers flexibility and keeping them honest.

Real Estate & Housing

Housing markets sit at the intersection of household finance and macroeconomics. I study how collateral constraints, local wealth shocks, and mortgage underwriting standards affect prices and volatility.

Bank Fragility & Intangible Capital

My recent work examines banks whose key asset is their depositor relationships — and what happens when those deposits become rate-sensitive. I also study how firms invest in intangible assets that are hard to measure and harder to value.

Featured Work

The Insurance is the Lemon: Failing to Index Contracts
with Benjamin Hébert · Journal of Finance, 2020

Why don't more financial contracts adjust for inflation or other risks? We show that adding insurance features to contracts can backfire — by attracting the riskiest borrowers, the insurance itself becomes a source of adverse selection. This helps explain why simple, non-indexed contracts persist even when they expose both sides to avoidable risk.

★ Brattle Group Prize, First Place
Are Lemons Sold First? Dynamic Signaling in the Mortgage Market
with Manuel Adelino & Kris Gerardi · Journal of Financial Economics, 2019 (Lead Article)

Conventional wisdom says the worst assets get dumped first, but the timing of loan sales tells a more nuanced story. We show that lenders strategically delay selling higher-quality loans to build a track record, letting early sales serve as costly signals. The data from U.S. mortgage markets strongly supports this dynamic signaling story.

★ Jensen Prize, Second Place
Capital Share Dynamics When Firms Insure Workers
with Hanno Lustig & Mindy Zhang Xiaolan · Journal of Finance, 2019

The share of income going to capital versus labor isn't just about technology — it's about risk sharing. We show that when firms insure workers against productivity shocks through sticky wages, the capital share becomes more volatile and procyclical. This has implications for understanding inequality and the distribution of economic risk.
